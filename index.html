The Adventures of
Barnaby Sebastian Rhodes
❦

The Magical Umbrella

❦ ❧ ❦
By W.C. Schmitz
Dedicated to Margot and Charlotte
Turn the page
I

Dear Margot and Charlotte,

Once upon a time, there lived a boy who dreamed every night, when he fell upon his soft and lumpy pillow, that he would be, one day, a great gentleman. His name is Barnaby Sebastian Rhodes, and he is about to turn eight years old!

There are very few occasions better than a birthday — and besides honey mustard on a chicken finger, nothing beats cookies-and-cream ice cream cake!

II

Well, Barnaby was about to discover there was indeed something better. On the morning of his eighth birthday, just as the sun stretched its golden arms over the rooftops, there came a peculiar knock at the door.

It was not the sort of knock made by a neighbor, or a postman, or anyone in a hurry.

“Barnaby! I think that knock is for you! Go check — who is knock, knock, knocking at the door?” Mom called from the hall.

So Barnaby scampered quick down the stairs, sliding into his blue checkered slippers just before reaching the big handle of the front door.

III

Barnaby opened the door. And there, standing quite tall and strong against the brick wall, like it had rung the bell itself, was an umbrella.

Not just for rainy days, not just for wind — a very handsome walking stick. A splendid umbrella with a gold ribbon tied with a note.

Barnaby untied the ribbon with unusual care, for it had the appearance of a ribbon that had travelled some distance to arrive precisely there.

Inside the note, written in a narrow well-practiced hand, were these words:

For Barnaby Sebastian Rhodes, upon the occasion of his eighth birthday.
IV

Beneath this, in smaller handwriting, it read:

Open, only when you are quite certain morning has begun.

Now Barnaby considered this carefully, for the morning had already begun in the ordinary sense, but it had not yet begun in any particularly interesting way.

So… he took a deep breath, held the umbrella upright with the wooden handle in his hand, and waited. And he waited, feeling quite silly holding an umbrella above his head on a sunny day.

V

“Surely, morning is ready to begin,” Barnaby said to himself.

And just like that, his finger found the button on the handle and the umbrella popped open — a tartan green dome wider than three dogs, enveloping the sky. Morning had now certainly begun.

And with it, something else. For there, printed inside the giant umbrella, Barnaby now saw — a map!

There was the path to the garage! The old stone birdbath! The big green hedge! And then he saw it — a small golden X that called his attention.

An X that… possibly… could it mean… Birthday Treasure?!

VI

Barnaby did not hurry. Golden X's, he felt quite certain, were not to be rushed toward. They were meant to be arrived at properly.

So he followed the path through the dew-swept grass, past the azaleas whose petals had gathered the morning about them like ruffled silk, until he came to the old stone birdbath, where the water was still, waiting for a beautiful blue jay to stop by.

There, beneath the surface, lay a small pale stone, shining softly, waiting to be found.

VII

And as Barnaby lifted the stone, a warm breeze whistled through the garden, catching the umbrella just enough to lift Barnaby from the ground.

Up and up he went. Over the hedge, past his bedroom window, and up, up, up over the shingles on the roof. Barnaby, looking left and right, could see the whole neighborhood! He could even see the bell in the church tower, and Mrs. Finnigan walking her small poodle dog.

As he soared and soared in the sky, Barnaby gripped his umbrella tight.

VIII

“Hello, Mrs. Finnigan! Hello, small poodle dog!” Barnaby shouted down from the sky.

“Barnaby! Barnaby! How did you get so high?!” Mrs. Finnigan yelled back.

“It is my birthday, Mrs. Finnigan! Today I am eight!”

“Oh my goodness! Eight is so great! Be safe, Barnaby!”

“Thank you, Mrs. Finnigan,” Barnaby said — because a true gentleman is always gregarious and gracious.

IX

From high above, Barnaby could see it all. Mom making fluffy pancakes with tall glasses of orange juice in the window. Grandpa in the car, his back seat full of birthday balloons. Dad on a walk with Remy, his curly dog.

Then, all of a sudden, Barnaby understood why the magical umbrella had found him.

It had not been sent to lead him away on a grand adventure. It was meant to show him that a birthday's real splendor is not found by flying farther and farther away, but by seeing that the day's real grandeur had never been above him at all.

X

And so Barnaby Sebastian Rhodes, on the morning of his eighth birthday, learned what every young gentleman ought to learn before he turns nine: that the world can look altogether different after you have risen a little above it — especially home.

And that the best gift is laughing and loving with the family that laughs and loves with you.

Barnaby gently pulled the umbrella closed and landed back in the garden, next to the old stone birdbath and the azaleas, right when Dad and Remy came home through the gate.

“Remy! Remy! Curly dog, you will never believe how small you look when you are flying high in the sky!”

“Oh, Barnaby, I am so glad you found the family umbrella! Happy birthday — it's going to be a great year,” Dad said.

And they walked into the kitchen for some of Mom's special pancakes.

— The End —
